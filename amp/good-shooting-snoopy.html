Good Shooting Snoopy

By Dusty R June 6, 2019

Good Shooting Snoopy, You Got the Red Barron said a CIA cable

The quote from a cable from the Director of the CIA at the time, William (Bill) Colby to Air America in Laos

Air America (airline) was used by the CIA and US government covertly to conduct military operations, posing as a civilian air carrier, in areas the US armed forces could not go due to treaty restraints contained in the 1954 and 1962 Geneva Accords. This was especially true for Laos. But both sides looked away and publicly denied having uniformed troops in Laos.

Air America  (AA) was the air taxi for the CIA's "Secret War in Laos". They transported everything, from people, food supplies, arms, ammo, live pigs and chickens, fuel, rice, medical supplies, and the list goes on. After a while, it was not such a secret war. AA flew helicopters, STOL (Short Takeoff and Landing) aircraft, transport aircraft, and more.

In August 1966, to support the escalating air war against the North Vietnamese Army (NVA), a Tactical Air Navigation (TACAN) station was first constructed on the mountain above Lima Site (for landing site) 85 in Laos by the CIA. A TACAN station allows fighter planes and bombers to fly in bad weather and help guide them to their targets more accurately and more safely. Plowing into a mountain during zero visibility is a really bad way to end your career.

On 12 January 1968, four North Vietnamese Air Force AN-2 Colt biplanes lifted off from an airfield in northeastern North Vietnam and headed west toward Laos. The aircraft were on a mission to destroy a US radar base that was guiding bombers in attacks against targets in North Vietnam. Known to the Americans as Site 85, the radar facility was perched atop a 5,800-foot high mountain, Phou Pha Thi. Manned by US Air Force volunteers “sheep dipped” as employees of the Lockheed Aircraft Corporation, the site had been in operation only a few months. The mountain, used for many years as a staging base for CIA-directed Hmong guerilla fighters and American special operations and rescue helicopters, was only 125 nautical miles from Hanoi. Air America, a CIA-proprietary, provided aerial support for the facility, the technicians, and the security forces.

The Colts reached Site 85 early in the afternoon, and two began bombing and strafing passes as the others circled nearby. Coincidentally, Air America captain Ted Moore, flying a UH-1D Huey helicopter carrying ammunition to the site, saw the attack

“It looked like World War I,” he recalled.

and gave chase to a Colt as it turned back to the Vietnamese border. Moore positioned his helicopter above the biplane, as crewman Glenn Woods fired an AK-47 rifle down on it. The pursuit continued for more than 20 minutes until the second AN-2 flew underneath the helicopter. Dropping back, Moore and Woods watched as the first AN-2 dropped and crashed into a ridge just west of the North Vietnamese border. Minutes later, the second Colt hit the side of a mountain three miles farther north. The other Colts escaped, inactive observers throughout. Within hours a CIA-controlled ground team reached the crashed aircraft and found bullet holes in the downed planes.

In the mists of the Annamite Mountains and part of a secret war, Air America employees Ted Moore and Glenn Woods gained the distinction of having shot down a fixed-wing aircraft from a helicopter, a singular aerial victory in the Vietnam War. Two months later, North Vietnamese commandos attacked and destroyed Site 85, inflicting the deadliest single ground loss of US Air Force personnel of the Vietnam War.

On 27 July 2007, CIA officially received An Air Combat First in an event attended by members of the Air America Board; pilot Ted Moore; Sawang Reed, the wife of flight mechanic Glenn Woods; CIA paramilitary legend Bill Lair; and the donors of the painting, former Air America officers Marius Burke and Boyd D. Mesecher.

The rest of the story is CIA legend. When DCI (Director of CIA) Bill Colby heard the news from the CIA Station in Vientiane Laos of the defense of LS 85 and the shoot down of two biplanes by Air America personnel in a helicopter, he then wrote a cable (like a secure email) back with basically this message:

Good shooting Snoopy, you got the Red Barron.

Colby was referring to the "Peanuts" comic strip where Snoopy was always flying his dog house and trying to shoot down an unseen enemy. In this case the Red Barron was the Russians / North Vietnamese / Communists.

My Father told me about this cable and the incident and it was so remarkable I remembered it for a long time.

Site 85 was then targeted for destruction by the North Vietnamese after the planes were shot down and over the coming weeks and months Site 85 was attacked, destroying the outpost, killing many Hmong soldiers and 11 Americans. The US Embassy called for an evacuation, it was too little and too late. It turned into a cluster fudge.
